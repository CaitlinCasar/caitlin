Casar et al. 2020

And I recently published a manuscript in Geobiology and will aim for at least two more publications before I graduate.

I mentioned this work is funded by NASA and the overarching objective is to try to learn something about habitability of rocky subsurface environments on other planets like Mars. So we’re studying the continental deep subsurface as an analog for these settings.

On Earth we know that fluid interactions with rock produce geochemical conditions that support microbial life, and these same sorts of processes could sustain life in the subsurface of Mars as well.

There’s been a global effort to characterize the habitability off the terrestrial subsurface which we now know hosts highly diverse and abundant life, and it was recently estimated that 30% of all prokaryotic life on Earth resides in the continental subsurface alone

Of this biomass, its estimated 20% is microbes suspended in fluids and as much as 80% is microbes attached to surfaces in biofilms. So there is a non-trival amount of microbial life in these systems on Earth and potentially on other planets, the majority of which is probably biofilms.

For this reason my work specifically focuses on biofilm communities - these are the microbes that attach themselves to rock surfaces. I am trying to shed light on how these biofilm communities are getting their energy and what the controls on biofilm diversity and biomass are here so we can better understand the habitability of rocky subsurface settings on other planets.

Mineral-hosted biofilms in the continental deep subsurface

  1. Who are the mineral-hosted microbes?
  2. How do they get their energy? - are they eating/breathing the minerals?
  3. What role do minerals play in controlling biofilm diversity and biomass?
This project focuses on mineral-hosted biofilms in the continental deep subsurface- specifically, (list off questions…) who are these microbes, how are they getting their energy, and what role do minerals play in controlling biofilm diversity and biomass?

Part of the reason there’s still unanswered questions about microbial life in the continental subsurface is because it’s challenging to get thousands of feet down into the crust. We take advantage of a gold mine in South Dakota where we’re able to access fracture fluids to a depth of 1.5km.

This is a borehole packer in the background, its basically a plastic pipe that allows us to sample fracture fluids. And you can see all this iron oxide that has precipitated out from all the dissolved ferrous in the fluids as it drips out and encounters air. The iron is coming from all the iron-rich minerals in the rock reacting with the fracture fluid, and its one of many energy sources dissolved in the fluids that can support microbial life.

Some of the microbes in these fractures float or swim around in the fluids, and there’s dissolved gases and ions in the fluids that they can use as energy sources, but the minerals in the rock can provide energy too

other microbes in the fracture can attach to the fracture surface, forming this thin, sticky film called a biofilm. Because they’re in direct contact with the rock, it might be easier for these microbes in the biofilm to access the minerals in the rock as an energy source.

and if they’re eating/breathing these minerals then we should probably see some differences in the number of microbes and the types of microbes colonizing the fracture surfaces

So the central question we wanted to address with this study is what role do minerals play in driving differences in biomass and biodiversity between the suspended and attached communities in the continental subsurface?

Thermodynamic Models

We can use what we know about the fracture fluid chemistry and host rock lithology to first do some thermo modelling to predict which minerals might be good energy sources at DeMMO.

This figure shows Gibb’s free energy on the x-axis with exergonic reactions to the right of the dotted line, and the various colors denote the different minerals we modeled with a the available dissolved electron donors and acceptors.

And you can see that all of these minerals can be energy yielding in the right reaction, but some seem to generally be better energy sources than others

for example e see that the majority of these are reactions with the a mn-oxide mineral called pyrolusite, so from this we can predict that pyrolusite could be a preferable electron acceptor for members of the biofilm communities, and we can test this out using field experiments to see if anything interesting happens when we introduce these minerals to the communities

To do this we set up field colonization reactors filled with either inert sand or single crystal minerals representative of the native rock. These reactors were connected to the outflow of a borehole packer and left for several moneths to allow time for biofilms to colonize the mineral surfaces. We then collected the reactors and estimated biomass using SEM on mineral coupons in the reactors and identified the members of the biofilms with DNA sequencing.

Diversity

we found that the fluid communities were more diverse than the biofilm communities, suggesting mineral and glass surfaces are selecting for a subset of the fluid communities

Communities

Different taxa dominated the biofilms vs. the fluid communities, with some taxa representing over 50% of the biofilm communities, indicating certain minerals promote colonization of these taxa over others

We also found that specific minerals enhance biomass. Here we’re looking at SEM images of pyrolusite coupons next to inert controls and you can see the densities and morphologies are pretty different between the two. So I collected 1300 of these images across all the experiments and estimated biomass for each.

Biomass

We found much higher cell densities in the biofilm communities compared to the fluid communities - as much as 3-4 orders of mag

The takeaway messages from this is that host rock mineralogy is probably an important control on biofilm diversity and biomass, where minerals can host taxonomically distinct communities of significantly greater biomass than the fluids in the continental subsurface, which has implications for the search for life on other planets like Mars.
